TOPIC: Diet and Lifestyle Guidance During the eMed Programme

GLP-1 medications are highly effective tools for weight loss, but they work best when combined with meaningful lifestyle changes. eMed's programme is designed around the principle that sustainable weight loss requires addressing four core pillars: nutrition, movement, sleep, and stress.

NUTRITION:
You do not need to follow a restrictive or highly regimented diet on the eMed programme. The goal is a balanced, sustainable approach to eating that supports the medication's effects rather than fighting against them.

General dietary recommendations include:

Balanced Diet:
Focus on whole foods — vegetables, lean proteins, legumes, wholegrains, and healthy fats. Minimise ultra-processed foods, refined sugars, and foods high in saturated fat.

Carbohydrate Awareness:
You do not need to eliminate carbohydrates. Instead, favour complex carbohydrates such as oats, brown rice, sweet potatoes, and legumes over refined carbs like white bread and sugary drinks. Being mindful of portion sizes helps manage post-meal blood sugar spikes.

Protein Intake:
Adequate protein helps preserve lean muscle mass during weight loss, which is important since GLP-1 medications reduce overall calorie intake significantly. Aim for a protein source at each meal — eggs, chicken, fish, tofu, Greek yoghurt, or pulses are all good options.

Managing Gastrointestinal Side Effects Through Diet:
- Eat smaller, more frequent meals rather than large ones
- Avoid fatty, fried, or very spicy foods, particularly in the early weeks
- Eat slowly and stop eating as soon as you feel full
- Stay well hydrated throughout the day

Fatty Foods:
Some patients experience worsening gastrointestinal side effects when consuming large amounts of fatty foods. Limiting high-fat meals can reduce nausea and discomfort, particularly during the dose escalation phase.

Alcohol:
Alcohol is a known risk factor for pancreatitis, and some GLP-1 medications may amplify this risk. Alcohol can also affect blood sugar levels and exacerbate side effects. Patients are advised to limit alcohol significantly during the programme, or to abstain entirely where possible.

PHYSICAL ACTIVITY:
Exercise is not a requirement to start the programme, but it plays an important role in long-term weight maintenance and overall health. General recommendations:

- Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week (e.g. brisk walking, cycling, swimming)
- Alternatively, 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity activity (e.g. running, intense cycling) achieves a similar benefit
- Include at least two sessions of muscle-strengthening activity per week
- Even small increases in daily movement — taking stairs, walking to the shops — make a meaningful difference

Exercise also helps preserve lean muscle mass during weight loss, improves insulin sensitivity, supports cardiovascular health, and has well-established benefits for mental health.

HYDRATION:
Some GLP-1 medications can lead to dehydration, particularly in the early weeks when nausea or vomiting may reduce fluid intake. Drinking adequate water throughout the day is important. Aim for at least 1.5 to 2 litres of water daily, and more if you are physically active or the weather is warm.

SLEEP:
Poor sleep is strongly associated with weight gain, insulin resistance, and increased appetite. The eMed programme recognises sleep as a core pillar of weight management. Patients struggling with sleep are encouraged to raise this with their clinician or access the mental health support available through the programme.

STRESS MANAGEMENT:
Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which can promote fat storage and drive cravings for high-calorie foods. The programme includes access to behavioural support and mental health resources to help patients manage stress as part of their wider weight loss journey.

SUPPLEMENTS:
Some patients benefit from supplementation during the programme, particularly Vitamin D (commonly deficient in the UK population), Vitamin B12 (which may be affected by dietary changes), and omega-3 fatty acids. Discuss any supplements with your clinician before starting.
