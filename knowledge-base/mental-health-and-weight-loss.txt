TOPIC: Mental Health and the Psychological Side of Weight Loss

Weight management is not purely a physical process. For many people, the relationship between their mental health, emotions, and eating behaviour is central to why weight loss has been difficult in the past. The eMed programme recognises this and incorporates mental health and behavioural support as core components of care.

THE PSYCHOLOGICAL DIMENSION OF WEIGHT:
Obesity and overweight are complex conditions influenced by genetics, environment, socioeconomic factors, and mental health. For many patients:
- Stress and anxiety lead to emotional eating or comfort eating
- Depression can reduce motivation to exercise and increase calorie intake
- Trauma and difficult life experiences can create unhealthy relationships with food
- Low self-esteem related to body image creates a cycle that is hard to break without support

None of these factors represent personal failings. They reflect the genuine complexity of weight management and the importance of addressing the psychological alongside the physical.

EFFECTS OF GLP-1 MEDICATION ON MOOD AND APPETITE:
Many patients report that GLP-1 medications reduce not just physical hunger but also food-related thoughts and cravings. This is because GLP-1 receptors are present in the brain, including in areas associated with reward and motivation. Patients often describe thinking about food less, feeling less compelled to snack, and finding it easier to make healthier choices.

This reduction in food preoccupation can have a positive effect on mental wellbeing — many patients report feeling more in control and less distressed about food than before starting the programme.

NURSE-LED MENTAL HEALTH SUPPORT:
eMed provides access to nurse-led mental health support as part of the programme. This is available to all patients and is particularly relevant for those who:
- Have a history of emotional or binge eating
- Experience anxiety or depression that affects their eating behaviour
- Find the psychological adjustment to weight loss challenging
- Struggle with motivation or self-belief

This support is not full psychiatric or psychological therapy, but it provides a professional, compassionate space to discuss the emotional aspects of the programme.

BODY IMAGE DURING WEIGHT LOSS:
Significant weight loss can bring unexpected psychological challenges. Some patients find that their feelings about their body do not automatically improve even as their weight decreases — a phenomenon sometimes called body image dissatisfaction. Others experience anxiety about loose skin, changes in social dynamics, or uncertainty about identity as their body changes.

These are normal experiences and worth discussing with the mental health support team or your clinician.

HAIR LOSS DURING WEIGHT LOSS:
Some patients notice increased hair shedding during rapid weight loss. This is a well-recognised phenomenon called telogen effluvium — a temporary disruption to the hair growth cycle caused by the physical stress of significant caloric restriction and rapid body change, rather than a direct effect of the medication. Hair shedding typically peaks around three to six months into the weight loss phase and resolves as the body adjusts. Adequate protein intake and nutrition can help minimise this effect.

EATING DISORDER HISTORY:
Patients with a history of eating disorders — including anorexia nervosa, bulimia, or binge eating disorder — should disclose this during the eligibility assessment. GLP-1 medications require careful consideration in this context. The clinical team will assess the appropriateness of treatment on an individual basis and may recommend additional mental health support.

BUILDING SUSTAINABLE HABITS:
The behavioural support element of the programme is designed to help patients develop skills and habits they will carry beyond the medication phase. This includes:
- Understanding hunger cues and eating mindfully
- Building a positive relationship with food and exercise
- Managing stress and emotional triggers
- Setting realistic, motivating goals
- Planning for maintenance after the active weight loss phase

SELF-COMPASSION AND PATIENCE:
Weight loss is rarely linear. Plateaus, slower weeks, and occasional setbacks are a normal part of the journey. eMed encourages patients to approach the programme with self-compassion — to recognise progress without fixating on perfection, and to reach out for support rather than withdrawing when things feel difficult.
